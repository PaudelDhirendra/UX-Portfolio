UX Research, UX & UI Design, User Testing

Remind ME

Remind Me was originally a Mobile Application work tool concept, an editable task schedular for busy professionals.
I was approached by a company - because their application is in development, their product and their name will remain anonymous for this case study at their request.
Tasked with designing a data driven advertising website for them, I was asked, "whether or not their application could be so much more than just another work tool?"
Using a wide array of User interaction, testing and application on their prototype, answered - yes it could and should be so much more!
This case study discovered that the target audience could be enhanced, further development opportunities arose that would help the Application succeed with the potential to become a worthy competitor for this market and a data driven designed website too to enhance their marketing campaign on release.

Case Summary

Product Vision

The company's vision was to ascertain whether there was any demand from the general public to enhance their target audience, focusing on:

  • How their product stacked up against the three main competitors currently on the market, who also had a To-Do type application released already.
  • To answer this I had to discover any possible development opportunities to make the application appeal to the general public, rather tha the original plan as solely a professional task manager tool.
  • Lastly, collect the data from this research and design a website around this data.

Discovered User Problem

It appeared that some aspects of their original plan were biased, in the sense that they did not include, or report, any user input throughout and so it was fair to infer that at this stage, was still in its infancy.
I highlighted my intentions to obtain some User input through public surveys and interviews ensuring total anonymity for the company, the product and the user was ethically upheld. This was to identify if there was any demand for their product from the general public, capture the user needs and emotions then design around the user and the business in a data driven method.

Outcomes

Tasked with designing the website to advertise the product depending on whether there was any demand for their product from the general public or more business orientated.
This was answered using public surveys and interviews, comparing the prototype against three similar market leading products available on the market. I was also able to create a data-driven SWOT analysis and user Personas from this data. Using the data I was carried out the Ideation Prioritisation on what was most important to show the Users that visited the website. Rapid Prototyping, Wireframes and Hi-Def designs were drafted and user tested for the website so that they were in the best possible position for the developers, the user and for the business.

Process Highlights

Project plan and responsibilities overview

Product Team

Hands on UX Researcher of the company's prototype/existing similar products and Designer of the Website as part of their Marketing Campaign.

Responsibilities

Hands on UX Researcher, Designer, UX Analyst and User Tester Facilitator for the Website Design.

Methodology

Focus Groups, Design Thinking, Think Aloud Protocol, Data Driven Design and Future Improvement Development.

Project Plan

Two week's discovery and ideation, followed by one week of rapid prototyping, one week of User Testing and one week final product design.

Discovery and Ideation

I began by researching the existing market on tailored Applications for task scheduling. After multiple forum reviews and questionnaires, 72 responses were gathered and 15 moderated interviews that were captured primarily via Social Media Networks and an online Survey. The data allowed me to paint a clearer picture of what the User's wanted from the product, what they expected and more importantly an accurate representation on who wanted the application. This information provided me with enough information to build a SWOT Analysis and User personas for further research.

What followed were efforts to map the existing user experiences to identify other opportunity developments:

SWOT Analysis and User Persona's

Discovery Analysis

Whilst interviewing the user's I ascertained that most of the applications available on the market were too complicated for the everyday user. This answered why the company had such a narrow target audience and aimed purely at professionals as a work tool. A suggestion that was accepted by the company was to make the HCI simpler and less 'cumbersome or business-like,' this would make it more appealable to the everyday user - thus expanding the target audience. I wasn't part of the design team for Application design, so defining it would have meant leveraging away from the scope of this case study, nevertheless, the SWOT Analysis findings highlighted areas for further product development opportunities, so that the application could appeal to the every day user too.

During the survey, I asked whether any of the participants had a similar application, which brand did they prefer and also which they did not like and why? Interviews were arranged through the survey, pain points were identified with our prototype and what the user would expect from the proposed website, this was so that I could paint a picture on a natural User flow for the site.

Idea Prioritisation

Based on discovery research findings I came up with three hypothesis to ensure successful product adoption. These covered:

  • Essential Security for the checkout areas.
  • Visual hierarchy for the product.
  • Simple and easily identifiable price section for the different versions of the product to enhance transparency and the trust from the user.
  • A visually pleasing and interactive review section.
  • A user friendly, safe and secure checkout section.
  • A user friendly contact section.

The features were listed and mapped based in technical complexity, potential Business and User efficiency values. The highest impact features were expected to be included in the developed product.

Hypothesis & Ideation

Data collected from the User Research and Discovery phase produced User expectations and Future Development Opportunities for further development of the application, which also gave more information on what to include for the website. This data along with expectations for the website design advanced Ideation into Hypothesis and Idea Prioritisation.

"Users spend most of their time on other sites. This means that users prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know."


Jakobs Law Of Internet User Experience - Jacob Nielson

Prototyping

Concentrating on high impact feature sets allowed me to create a story that could be split into a few major user scenarios: Product Identity, simple HCI and familiarity inspired by the product itself and intrigue from the competitive research. Focusing on the natural user flow, the usability and accessibility of the website, whilst keeping the focus on branding and the business advertisement requirements, the following steps in designing the website followed.

Competitive Research

Using UX tools to gain some insights on what the competition's SEO keywords were, how they ranked with other competitors, spot new opportunities, show the exact content needed and revealed design patterns that are collectively successful for the user, product and business.

Natural User Flow

Data recorded during User Interviews and the public Surveys enabled me to capture and encapsulate the users feelings towards the products tested and through the data recorded during the interviews, now in Persona's, I was able to produce a Naturel User Flow for the website.

Rapid Prototyping

During Rapid Prototyping, several iterations generated during a short period of time, the features were created and tested in an attempt to optimize characteristics like shape, size, and overall usability based on user feedback and analysis. Final tests produced the final prototype and the Natural User Flow had been established.

Hi-Def Designs

Once the final iteration of the wireframes were acknowledged after User testing, all features and functionality was transferred to Hi-Def Designs using Adobe XD and Illustrator for the icons. These were tested using Think Aloud Protocol, pain points were corrected and final designs were completed.

Validation

The prototype has been tested on 15 users (5 for each of the created persona's), in order to to validate my Hypothesis. Using the previously determined User Flow to guide the User through the prototype. User's paralinguistic features were recorded using a web-cam and video recording software at the agreement of the participant, they were tested ethically a new set of participants were brought in for the second wave of testing. The identity of the participants shall remain anonymised as agreed.

From the previously conducted survey, willing participants tested the prototype created for mobile and desktop viewport's using the Think Aloud Protocol. Asking a series of open ended questions the test administrator was able to understand how the user's felt about the product whilst also testing the age ranges specified earlier to expand the customer base.

    Earlier Tested Designs.

  • The early prototypes proved that the pre-determined User flow was correct.
  • Primary and Secondary buttons were required for some of the features, such as the video sections for example. First wave of testing proved that having one button for all was confusing.
  • Early designs were shown to have too much text content which delayed the user from efficiently moving through to complete tasks.
  • The initial checkout design proved my hypothesis to be correct by adding different payment methods for the user.
  • Color Psychology, Emphasis on SEO keywords such as, "Risk Free" and offering Credit Card Security increased efficiency and a positive emotion.
  • The majority of User's found a scrollable review section cumbersome and preferred a more modern "swipeable" section instead. Designing the new carousel became priority.
  • A/B testing on the review section followed, one was scrollable and the other was swipeable.

    Final Tested Designs.

    All Feedback was analysed, errors were caught and designs improved greatly thanks to this. I observed almost entirely positive feedback from the final designs.

  • User's found the new buttons easier to use and determined the importance of each swiftly.
  • Analysis shown that the majority of the users interacted and spent more time using the swipeable version of the review section.
  • All participants were able to complete the checkout section with ease and return back to the home screen.
  • 99% of the users were not showing signs of confusion or stress whilst completing the test for the final version.

Outcomes

Based on the findings from both the UX research and the Design validation sessions I was able to produce a list of changes that were addressed and updated in the prototype along with any potential future alterations for the Application passed onto the company.
It was realised during the Validation stage that all of the user's became concerned when they clicked on the video section of the design and wondered why the video didn't play. It was mentioned prior to testing that there was no promotional video provided as the company wanted their application to appeal to the general public and at that moment in time was developed as businesses tool. They did not want to confuse the participants by advertising something that it wasn't going to be, so a decision on their part was to not include the video. Another thing was because of the potential improvements found was significantly different to the application that they had, some of the details in the designs such as descriptive text will change once those improvements are developed.
